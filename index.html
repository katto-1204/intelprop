Slide 04 · Introduction

WHAT IS IP?

Intellectual Property (IP) refers to legal rights granted over creations of the mind — inventions, literary and artistic works, designs, symbols, names, images, and software.

IP protection ensures creators can control and benefit from their innovations while promoting economic growth and technological advancement.
3
Core Types
20
Patent Yrs
+70
Copyright Yrs
Who It CoversInventors, authors, artists, developers, designers, and organizations.
Why It MattersPromotes innovation, protects competitive advantage, attracts investors.
In ITEvery line of code, app name, UI design, and software patent is governed by IP law.
Core FrameworkCopyright (C), Patent (P), Trademark (T) — the CPT Framework.
Slide 05 · C · P · T — Type 1

Copyright

Protects original works of authorship. Automatic upon creation — no registration needed. Covers expression of ideas, not the ideas themselves.

Source Code
Books
Music
Design
Websites
Cheap, easy to obtain, lasts a long time. Allows fair dealing of IP.
Duration: Life of author + 70 years  ·  Cost: Free

KEY FACTS

  • Automatic — no registration required
  • Protects expression, not underlying ideas
  • Allows fair dealing of intellectual property
  • In IT: copying code without permission = infringement
  • International protection via the Berne Convention
Slide 06 · C · P · T — Type 2

Patent

Protects inventions or technical solutions, granting exclusive rights. Expensive and difficult to obtain — but allows licensing to other developers.

Requirements: Novelty · Non-obviousness · Industrial Applicability · Registration required
Duration: ~20 years in Canada & USA

TYPES OF PATENTS

U
Utility Patents
Protect new processes, machines, articles of manufacture, or compositions of matter.
D
Design Patents
Protect the ornamental or aesthetic design of a functional product.
P
Plant Patents
Protect new and distinct varieties of asexually reproduced plants.
Slide 07 · C · P · T — Type 3

Trademark

Protects brand identifiers — names, logos, slogans, product names, and symbols. Prevents consumer confusion and protects brand identity.

Business Names
Logos
Slogans
Symbols
Trademarks can last indefinitely as long as they remain in active use and registration is maintained.

KEY FACTS

  • Prevents consumer confusion in the market
  • Protects brand identity and market reputation
  • Can exist as common law or registered trademark
  • Registration recommended for full legal protection
  • Must conduct clearance search before launch
Slide 08 · Comparison

Should software be Copyrighted or Patented? Copyright protects the code expression (cheap, automatic). Patent protects the invention/algorithm (expensive, 20 yrs). Most companies use both.
Slide 09 · Fair Use & Copyright Warnings

FAIR USE

Fair Use allows use of copyrighted material without permission under specific conditions — commentary, criticism, education, or parody.

Education
Teaching, research, and academic study.
Commentary
Critiquing or commenting on a work.
Parody
Imitating a work for comic/satirical effect.
Research
Non-commercial scholarly analysis.

WHY FACEBOOK ADDS COPYRIGHT WARNINGS

Not a legal defense

Slide 10 · Key Distinction

COPYRIGHT VS
COPYLEFT

Copyright
  • Creator holds ALL exclusive rights
  • Others need permission to use, copy, or distribute
  • Default protection — automatic upon creation
  • Can be licensed commercially for profit
  • Examples: proprietary software, books, films
Copyleft
  • Free to use, modify, and share
  • Derivative works MUST remain open under same license
  • Keeps software permanently free — "share alike"
  • Example: GNU General Public License (GPL)
  • Freedom with responsibility
Using GPL code in a commercial product may legally require you to release your entire codebase as open-source.

Slide 13 · Academic Ethics

WHAT IS PLAGIARISM?

Plagiarism is the act of presenting another person's work, ideas, or expressions as one's own without proper acknowledgment. It violates both academic integrity and copyright law.

In software: copying proprietary code or failing to acknowledge open-source contributors can lead to serious legal disputes.
7
Forms
Zero
Tolerance

CONSEQUENCES

AC
Academic: Failing Grade
Immediate zero, suspension, or expulsion.
PR
Professional: Job Loss
Termination, legal liability, and blacklisting.
LG
Legal: Civil / Criminal
Copyright infringement — lawsuits and damages.
Slide 14 · 7 Forms

FORMS OF PLAGIARISM

Direct Plagiarism
Copy-pasting text verbatim without citation.
Paraphrasing
Rewriting another's ideas without attribution.
Self-Plagiarism
Reusing one's own previous work without disclosure.
Mosaic Plagiarism
Blending paraphrased and copied text from multiple sources.
Purchased Work
Submitting downloaded or purchased assignments as original.
Code Plagiarism
Reusing code without attribution to the original author.
AI Plagiarism
Presenting AI-generated content as entirely one's own where prohibited.
Slide 15 · IP Issues

TRADEMARK INFRINGEMENT

DefinitionUsing a mark identical or confusingly similar to a registered trademark without authorization.
ExamplesCreating a similar logo, selling counterfeit goods, mimicking product packaging.
Legal Standard"Likelihood of confusion" among consumers is the determining test.
PenaltiesCourt injunctions, monetary damages, forced rebranding, destruction of goods.
Parody DefenseClear satire may be protected — if no consumer confusion is possible.
Search the IP Philippines Trademark Database before naming your app or company.
Slide 16 · Parody in Branding

PARODY & BRANDS

Parody is protected when it is clearly satirical and not likely to cause consumer confusion. Would a reasonable person actually be confused?

Original Brand
Facebook

Global social media platform. Trademark protected since 2004.

VS
Parody
FACEBOOL

Local fishball store. Clearly satirical — no confusion possible.

Original Brand
7-Eleven

Global convenience chain. Trademark registered worldwide.

VS
Parody
7 Evelyn

Local sari-sari store joke. No reasonable person would confuse it with a major chain.

Parody works as a legal defense when satirical intent is unmistakably obvious. Genuine consumer confusion = infringement.
Slide 17 · Cybersquatting

WHAT IS CYBERSQUATTING?

Cybersquatting is the practice of registering, trafficking in, or using a domain name that is identical or confusingly similar to a trademark, with the bad-faith intent to profit from the trademark owner's goodwill.

Illegal under the ACPA in the US and the UDRP internationally.
5
Types
UDRP
Legal Remedy
Common MotiveRegistering "google-official.com" then demanding $50,000 from Google to buy it back.
Who It HarmsTrademark holders, consumers, and legitimate businesses.
UDRPUniform Domain Dispute Resolution Policy — allows trademark holders to recover domains internationally.
Best PracticeRegister your domain and common variations early — before launching any product or brand.
Slide 18 · Cybersquatting — Types

TYPES OF CYBERSQUATTING

Classic Cybersquatting
Registering a trademark as a domain to sell it back at inflated price.
Typosquatting
Registering misspelled brand domains (e.g., "gooogle.com") to capture traffic.
Reverse Hijacking
Filing a bad-faith dispute to seize a legitimately registered domain.
Combosquatting
Combining a trademark with other words (e.g., "paypal-support.com").
Homograph Attacks
Using visually similar characters (Cyrillic) to mimic a legitimate domain.
Legal Fix: UDRP
International mechanism for domain recovery by trademark holders.

Slide 21 · Ethics & Legal Duties

ETHICAL & LEGAL DUTIES

Responsible IP Practices

  • Cite all sources properly in academic and professional work
  • Review software licenses before using, modifying, or distributing code
  • Register original trademarks before launching products
  • Avoid unauthorized use of protected images, music, and software
  • Conduct IP audits and implement confidentiality policies

Preventive Measures

  • Use detection tools — Turnitin, Grammarly, MOSS for code
  • Maintain documentation of original work with timestamps
  • Consult legal professionals before product launches
  • Use SCA tools for open-source license compliance
  • Establish internal IP policies and training programs
